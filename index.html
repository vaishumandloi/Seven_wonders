The Glimps of Seven Wonders

1. Taj Mahal

Taj Mahal- Agra, India

The Taj Mahal is white marble mausoleum complex in Agra, western Uttar Pradesh state, northern India. The Taj Mahal was built by the Mughal emperor Shah Jahan to immortalize his beloved wife Mumtaz, who died in childbirth in 1631, having been the emperor’s inseparable companion since their marriage in 1612. India’s most famous and widely recognized building, it is situated on the bank of the river Yamuna. Taj Mahal is also known as a symbol of love. It took 22 years and about 22,000 workers constructed the Taj Mahal. Taj Mahal is India’s most famous and widely recognized building. It is distinguished as the finest example of Mughal architecture, a perfect blending of Indian, Persian, and Islamic styles. Other attractions include twin mosque buildings (placed symmetrically on either side of the mausoleum), lovely gardens, and a museum.

2. Petra

Petra- Jordan

Petra is one of the oldest cities in the world. The name 'Petra' means 'Rocks'. Petra is fomous for its rock-cut architecture and water conduit system, Petra is also called the "Rose City" because of the colour of the stone from which it is carved. Petra is located in the southwestern corner of the Hashemite Kingdom of Jordon. Petra is half-built, half-carved into the rock, and is surrounded by mountains riddled with passages and gorges. It is one of the world's most famous archaeological sites, where ancient Eastern traditions blend with Hellenistic architecture. One of the most famous structures in Petra is Al Khazneh. It is a stone building that accommodated more than 20,000 people. At the beginning of the 8th century A.D., Petra was mostly abandoned and had lost its significance. Its unique architecture continues to make it stand out and this caught the eye of archaeologists and historians.

3. The Great Wall of China

The Great Wall of Chine- Beijing, China

The Great Wall is the largest man-made project in the world. The complete route is over 20,000 km, stretching from the east seaside to the west desert in northern China, winding up and down across mountains and plateaus like a dragon. It took over 2,000 years to construct the wall. The Great Wall is not a single-structured wall, it includes beacon towers, barriers, barracks, garrison stations, and fortresses along the walls, together forming an integrated defense system. It is Chinese people's greatest cultural icon. The wall was built with stones and earth, a more important part of the construction was the glutinous rice. The sticky rice was used as the mortar due to its adhesive property. Studies have shown that the sticky substance of the rice is behind the endurance and strength of the wall. The Great Wall of China is made up of 19 separate walls joined to form one massive wall that we know today because it is made by different-different provinces according to their convenience.

4. Chichen Itza

Chichen Itza- Mexico

Chichen Itza is an ancient Mayan city in Mexico. This site is home to the remnants of many ancient Mayan structures, including the famed El Castillo pyramid that dominates the site’s center. El Castillo is the largest and most famous pyramid at Chichen Itza and 98 feet in height and can not be climbed. At its peak, Chichen Itza was the most populous city in the entire Yucatan Peninsula. Even today, its ruins reflect a great civilization filled with architectural marvels and ancient secrets.

5. Christ the Redeemer

Christ the Redeemer- Rio de Janeiro, Brazil

Christ the Redeemer is a statue of Jesus Christ, located in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil. It sits atop Mount Corcovado, which means “hunchback” in Portuguese. Christ the Redeemer is 30 metres (98 feet) high, excluding its 8-metre (26 feet) pedestal. The statue’s arms stretch 28 metres (92 feet) wide. It weighs 635 metric tons and is the one of the largest statue in the world. It took nine years to build, Construction started in 1922 and finishing in 1931.

6. Machu Picchu

Machu Picchu- Peru

Machu Picchu is more than 7,000 feet above sea level in the Andes Mountains in Peru. In the Quechua Indian language, “Machu Picchu” means “Old Peak” or “Old Mountain.” It is a symbol of the Incan Empire. Above Machu Picchu is a 'No-Fly' Zone. Each Stone which is used in Machu Picchu is cut so precisely that the most sacred constructions were built without the use of mortar. The structures were so well built with a technique called Ashlar (stones that are cut to fit together without mortar) that not even a knife blade can fit in between stones. Although many of the stones that were used to build the city were more than 50 pounds, it is believed that no wheels were used to transport these rocks up the mountain. Rather, it is thought that hundreds of men pushed the heavy rocks up the steep mountain side.

7. Colosseum

Colosseum- Rome, Italy

The Colosseum is an oval amphitheatre in the centre of the city of Rome, Italy. It is the largest ancient amphitheatre ever built. Colosseum built between 72 AD and 80 AD, it consists of stone and concrete. It was constructed by tens of thousands of Jewish slaves under the rule of Emperor Vespasian. Colosseum is 189 metres long, 156 metres wide and 50 metres in height. Such famous venues as the Colosseum and Circus Maximus of Rome would host events involving magnificent processions, exotic animals, gladiator battles, chariot races, executions and even mock naval battles.
